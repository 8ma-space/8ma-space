Today's practice is different. It's not about discharging stress in the moment — it's about building a rhythm that keeps the nervous system from accumulating it in the first place.

I call it the Stability Check-in. It takes two minutes and works best if you anchor it to something you already do every day — morning coffee, the first minute at your desk, or just before you close your laptop at the end of the day.

Today's Practice — The Stability Check-in

At your chosen anchor moment, pause for two minutes and ask yourself three questions:

1. Where is the pressure right now?
Scan your body briefly — chest, jaw, shoulders, stomach. Just notice without judgment.

2. What is the pressure actually about?
Name it specifically. Not "everything" — one specific thing. The conversation you're avoiding, the decision you're delaying, the responsibility you're carrying silently.

3. What is one small thing I can do to not add to this today?
Not fix it. Not solve it. Just one small action that doesn't make it worse — or one thing you can consciously set down, even temporarily.

That's it. Two minutes. The same questions, every day.

Here's why this matters more than it sounds. Chronic pressure doesn't usually come from dramatic events — it accumulates through a hundred small moments of not checking in, not naming what's happening, and not making deliberate choices about what you're carrying. This practice creates a daily interruption in that accumulation.

Done consistently, this is the kind of shift that compounds. In a week, you start noticing patterns. In a month, you have language for what's happening in your body. In three months, you're making decisions from a fundamentally different baseline.

You now have three tools — the Pressure Map, the 4-6 Breath, and the Stability Check-in. They work best together, but any one of them used consistently will start to change your baseline.
